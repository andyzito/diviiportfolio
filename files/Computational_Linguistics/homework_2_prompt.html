Part 3: Calculate cross-entropy and perplexity

You used the training data to create a simple probabilistic model of your tweet corpus. Now you will evaluate how good a model of your tweet corpus this is; you should have some intuitive sense of how good a model it is on the basis of the previous exercise!

We would like you to:

  • Write a function that takes a training set and a probability model, and returns the cross entropy.
  • Write a function that takes a training set and a probability model, and returns the perplexity.

Recall that the cross entropy is a way of measuring the average surprise of each event in your test data, given a probability model. The events in our test data are the individual characters. Cross entropy for a probability model m based on training data L is calculated by:

$$ H(L,m) = -\sum_{i=1}^N \dfrac{1}{N} \log_2 m(x_i) $$

Where N is the number of events in your test data, and $$$m(x_i)$$$ is the probability of the i-th event $$$x_i$$$ under the estimated model m. The perplexity of a model is given by:

$$ 2^{-\sum_{i=1}^N \dfrac{1}{N} \log_2 m(x_i)} $$

Once you've written these functions, apply them to your test data, and calculate the cross entropy and the perplexity of your test data. Interpret the results, being sure to answer the following questions: what are the units of cross entropy, and what does the value you get mean in intuitive terms? What are the units of perplexity, and what does the value you get mean in intuitive terms? How do your estimates compare to Shannon's estimated cross entropy for a zeroth order model of English characters; are they better or worse? Why? Use Manning and Schütze Chapter 2's discussion of Information Theory as your reference.
